What is Tubal Surgery?

Tubal surgery refers to surgical procedures performed on the fallopian tubes to restore their normal function. The fallopian tubes are essential for natural conception as they transport eggs from the ovaries to the uterus and provide the site where fertilization occurs.

When tubes are blocked, damaged, or scarred due to infection, endometriosis, or previous surgery, tubal surgery can help restore their function. Modern techniques use minimally invasive approaches that reduce recovery time and improve outcomes.

Types of Tubal Surgery Procedures

Tubal Reanastomosis

Reconnecting fallopian tubes after tubal ligation (sterilization reversal) to restore fertility.

Salpingostomy

Creating a new opening in the fallopian tube when the end is blocked (hydrosalpinx).

Adhesiolysis

Removing scar tissue and adhesions around the fallopian tubes and ovaries.

Fimbrioplasty

Reconstructing the fimbrial end of the tube to improve egg pickup function.

Salpingectomy

Removing severely damaged tubes that may interfere with IVF success.

Tubal Cannulation

Non-surgical procedure to clear proximal tubal blockages using a catheter.

Who Needs Tubal Surgery?

Tubal Blockage

Women with blocked fallopian tubes due to infection, endometriosis, or previous surgery.

Hydrosalpinx

Fluid-filled, blocked fallopian tubes that can reduce IVF success rates.

Sterilization Reversal

Women who want to restore fertility after tubal ligation.

Pelvic Adhesions

Scar tissue around tubes and ovaries affecting normal function.

Ectopic Pregnancy History

Previous ectopic pregnancies that may have damaged tubal function.

PID History

Previous pelvic inflammatory disease causing tubal damage.

Success Rates & Outcomes

Tubal Reanastomosis

70-85%

Pregnancy rates after sterilization reversal in appropriate candidates.

Adhesiolysis

40-60%

Pregnancy rates after removal of pelvic adhesions.

Salpingostomy

20-40%

Pregnancy rates after creating new tubal openings.

The Surgical Process

Pre-operative Assessment

Comprehensive evaluation including imaging studies and fertility testing.

Minimally Invasive Surgery

Laparoscopic approach with small incisions for faster recovery.

Recovery Period

Short hospital stay with gradual return to normal activities.

Follow-up Care

Regular monitoring and fertility tracking after surgery.

Benefits

  • Potential for natural conception
  • Avoids need for IVF in some cases
  • Minimally invasive techniques
  • Shorter recovery time
  • Cost-effective long-term solution
  • Improves IVF success rates when indicated

Considerations

  • Success depends on extent of damage
  • Risk of ectopic pregnancy
  • General surgical risks
  • May not restore full function
  • Age affects success rates
  • Alternative treatments may be needed
